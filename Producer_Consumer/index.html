Producer
Consumer Problem

The producer-consumer problem is a classic synchronization problem in operating systems. It involves two types of processes: producers, which generate data, and consumers, which consume the data. The challenge is to ensure that producers and consumers can work concurrently and efficiently without data corruption or resource conflicts.

SJF

Shortest Job
First

SJF is a non-preemptive process scheduling algorithm that selects the process with the smallest execution time next. It aims to minimize the average waiting time and response time, prioritizing shorter processes over longer ones.

  • Simulation
RR

Round
Robin

RR is a preemptive process scheduling algorithm that allocates a fixed time quantum to each process in a cyclic manner. Processes are executed in a round-robin fashion, allowing fairness and preventing starvation, but potentially incurring higher context-switching overhead.

  • Simulation
PP

Priority
Preemptive

Priority preemptive scheduling assigns priorities to processes and allows higher priority processes to preempt lower priority ones. It ensures that higher priority processes are given preference in CPU allocation, but may lead to lower priority processes waiting indefinitely.

  • Simulation
PNP

Priority
Non Preemptive

Priority non-preemptive scheduling assigns priorities to processes without preemption. Once a process starts executing, it continues until it completes or blocks, regardless of higher priority processes becoming available. It may result in longer response times for higher priority processes if lower priority processes are executing for extended periods.
